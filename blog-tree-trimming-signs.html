5 Signs Your Trees Need
Professional Trimming Now

📅 Feb 10, 2026 · 🕑 4 min read · By NPLawn Expert Team

Trees are one of the greatest assets a property can have — they add value, shade, privacy, and beauty. But they're also one of the most neglected. Unlike a shaggy lawn that's visually obvious, tree problems often develop quietly until they become dangerous or expensive. Here are five signs that your trees need professional attention now, not next season.

Sign 1: Dead, Dying, or Hanging Branches

Dead branches — identifiable by their brittle bark, lack of leaves in spring and summer, or wood that snaps rather than bends — are a serious hazard. They can fall without warning, especially during wind, ice storms, or heavy rain. A branch that's already disconnected from the living wood of the tree but still hanging (called a "widow maker") is the most dangerous kind, since any vibration can send it down.

What to look for:

  • Branches with no leaves when surrounding branches are leafed out
  • Bark that is missing, shrunken, or peeling away from the wood
  • Visible cracks or splits in large limbs
  • Branches that sag significantly lower than they used to

"Dead branches don't get better on their own. Every season you wait, the wood becomes more brittle and the fall risk increases."

Sign 2: Crossing or Rubbing Limbs

When two branches rub against each other, they create wounds that strip bark and expose the living tissue underneath. These wounds become entry points for fungal disease, insects, and bacteria. Over time, the weaker of the two branches often dies entirely — or both do.

Crossing limbs are also a structural problem. As they grow, they can compete for the same space, causing the canopy to become dense and uneven. This increases wind resistance during storms, raising the risk of the whole canopy failing.

Professional trimming removes the weaker or less desirable of the two branches, allowing the remaining one to grow freely and closing off the wound before disease sets in.

Sign 3: Uneven or Lopsided Canopy

A healthy tree grows with a balanced canopy that distributes weight evenly around the trunk. When one side grows significantly heavier — due to a dominant branch, proximity to a structure, or previous improper pruning — the tree becomes mechanically stressed. The trunk and root system have to compensate for the uneven load, and in severe cases the whole tree can lean and eventually fail.

An uneven canopy is also an aesthetic problem, but more importantly it's a structural one. Crown balancing — selectively removing branches to redistribute weight — is a skilled job that requires understanding the tree's architecture, not just cutting until it "looks right."

⚠️

Important: Never top a tree (removing the entire upper canopy) as a shortcut to reduce height. Topping destroys the tree's natural structure, triggers rapid regrowth of weak, poorly attached shoots, and significantly shortens the tree's lifespan. It also looks terrible.

Sign 4: Branches Too Close to Your Home, Power Lines, or Other Structures

As a rule of thumb, tree branches should maintain at least 10 feet of clearance from your roofline, and nothing should touch or overhang utility lines (that's a job for your utility company or a certified arborist with line-clearance certification). Branches that brush against the roof or siding cause:

  • Shingle damage from abrasion during windy conditions
  • Moisture accumulation that accelerates rot and invites mold
  • A direct highway for squirrels, raccoons, and insects to access your attic
  • Clogged gutters from leaves and debris

If you can hear branches on your roof during a storm, it's been too long since your last trim.

Sign 5: Sucker Growth and Water Sprouts

Suckers are fast-growing, vertical shoots that emerge from the base of the trunk or from the rootstock below a graft point. Water sprouts are the same type of growth but appearing along major branches. Both are symptoms of stress — the tree is trying to rapidly regenerate leaf mass — and both are structurally weak, poorly attached, and resource-draining.

Left unchecked, suckers can grow into full branches in a single season and significantly alter the shape and structure of the tree. They should be removed as soon as they appear — ideally in spring before they harden.

Why DIY Tree Trimming Is Riskier Than You Think

A pruning saw and a YouTube video aren't the same as years of training. Tree trimming hazards include:

  • Falling branches — trajectory is hard to predict, especially in a confined yard
  • Working at height with improper equipment
  • Improper cuts that damage the branch collar, leaving the tree vulnerable to disease
  • Removing too much at once, stressing the tree into decline
