The Science

Why Your Lawn Needs Aeration

Heavy foot traffic, clay soil, and normal use compact soil over time. Compaction blocks water, air, and nutrients from reaching roots — even with great mowing and fertilizing.

Breaks Up Compaction

Hollow tines pull out plugs of soil, creating channels for air, water, and nutrients to flow deep into the root zone.

Boosts Root Growth

Deeper root systems mean a lawn that's more drought-tolerant, disease-resistant, and vibrant green.

Reduces Thatch

Aerating accelerates thatch breakdown by introducing beneficial soil microbes throughout the lawn.

Seeding Success

New seed dropped into open aeration holes germinates faster and at a far higher rate than surface seeding alone.

Best Time to Aerate

Timing Makes All the Difference

The ideal window depends on your grass type. We'll tell you exactly when your lawn is ready.

Cool-Season Grasses

Kentucky Bluegrass, Fescue, Ryegrass — best aerated in early fall (Sept–Oct) when temps drop below 70°F.

Warm-Season Grasses

Bermuda, Zoysia, St. Augustine — best aerated in late spring (May–June) as they enter active growth.

Spring Touch-Up

Overseeding thin spots in spring after soil temps reach 50°F — a great way to fill gaps without full aeration.
