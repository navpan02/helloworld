Designing a Low-Maintenance Landscape
That Still Looks Amazing

📅 Jan 24, 2026 · 🕑 7 min read · By NPLawn Expert Team

The most common complaint we hear from homeowners isn't that their yard looks bad — it's that maintaining it consumes their entire weekend. Hours of mowing, trimming, weeding, and watering add up fast, and by summer's end, the yard starts to feel like a second job rather than a place to relax.

The good news: low-maintenance landscaping is not the same as boring landscaping. With the right plant choices, smart layout decisions, and a few permanent improvements, you can have a yard that genuinely turns heads while demanding only a fraction of the time you're spending now.

1. Start with a Soil Test — Really

This step gets skipped most often, and it's the reason so many landscaping projects fail within two years. A soil test (about $15–$30 through your county extension office) tells you your soil's pH, organic matter content, and which nutrients are deficient. Plants placed in soil that matches their needs thrive with minimal intervention. Plants fighting their environment need constant supplementing.

In general: most ornamental plants prefer a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. If you're in a region with heavy clay or overly sandy soil, amending it before you plant is far cheaper than struggling to keep plants alive afterward.

2. Right Plant, Right Place

This is the single most powerful principle in low-maintenance landscaping. A plant that's perfectly suited to its location — the right sun exposure, moisture level, and soil type — will essentially take care of itself once established. A plant fighting its environment requires constant intervention.

"The right plant in the right place needs almost no help. The wrong plant needs everything — and still looks bad."

Before buying anything, assess each planting area:

  • Sun — full sun (6+ hrs), part sun (3–6 hrs), or shade (<3 hrs)?
  • Moisture — does the area dry out quickly, stay consistently moist, or flood after rain?
  • Space — what's the mature spread of this plant, not just its nursery size?

Oversized plants planted in tight spaces become a constant pruning problem. Always plan for the mature size, not the size in the pot.

3. Go Native Wherever Possible

Native plants are the ultimate low-maintenance choice because they evolved in your specific climate, soil type, and rainfall pattern. Once established (typically 1–2 seasons), most native plants need little to no supplemental watering, no fertilizing, and minimal pest control.

For the Midwest, excellent native choices include:

  • Prairie dropseed — elegant ornamental grass for sunny borders
  • Wild bergamot (Monarda fistulosa) — purple blooms, pollinator magnet
  • Black-eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta) — bright yellow flowers through summer and fall
  • Serviceberry (Amelanchier) — four-season interest: spring flowers, summer berries, fall color
  • Switchgrass (Panicum virgatum) — structural grass that holds winter interest

4. Mulch is Your Best Friend

A 2–3 inch layer of organic mulch in all planting beds is one of the best investments in a low-maintenance landscape. Here's what it does:

  • Suppresses weed germination, reducing weeding by 70–80%
  • Retains soil moisture, reducing watering frequency by 25–50%
  • Regulates soil temperature, protecting roots in heat and cold
  • Breaks down slowly to improve soil structure and feed beneficial microbes
  • Gives planting beds a clean, finished look year-round

Keep mulch 2–3 inches away from plant stems and tree trunks to prevent rot. Refresh mulch every 1–2 years as it decomposes — you're adding organic matter to your soil at the same time.

💡

Pro tip: Shredded hardwood bark mulch or wood chip mulch breaks down more slowly and improves soil more than dyed mulch, which can contain contaminants and decomposes faster.

5. Reduce Lawn Area Strategically

Turf grass is the single most labor-intensive element of most residential landscapes. Mowing, edging, fertilizing, aerating, watering — it adds up. Converting some or all of your lawn to lower-maintenance alternatives can dramatically reduce your workload:

  • Groundcovers — creeping thyme, pachysandra, vinca, or native sedges grow dense enough to crowd out weeds and need little to no mowing
  • Perennial borders — a wide mixed border of perennials and shrubs replaces a strip of lawn that requires regular edging
  • Gravel or decomposed granite — excellent for dry, sunny strips that struggle to support grass
  • Rain gardens — convert a low, wet area that drowns turf into a planted depression that manages stormwater naturally

You don't have to eliminate all your lawn. Reducing turf in the hardest-to-maintain areas — narrow strips, slopes, shaded zones, areas near water features — yields the biggest reduction in effort.

6. Smart Hardscaping

Patios, paths, and defined edges don't grow and don't need water. Expanding hardscaped areas around the house, along fences, or in high-traffic zones reduces the turf that needs weekly care and creates defined outdoor living spaces.

Key considerations for low-maintenance hardscaping:

  • Use permeable materials (gravel, permeable pavers) where possible to manage stormwater
  • Install proper edging between hardscaping and planting beds to prevent grass from creeping in
  • Choose materials that age gracefully — natural stone and concrete look better over time, while some composite materials can fade or warp

7. Irrigation That Works With You

Hand-watering is one of the most time-consuming maintenance tasks. A drip irrigation system in planting beds delivers water directly to root zones — no evaporation, no wet foliage, minimal waste. Combined with a smart timer or soil moisture sensor, a drip system waters only when plants actually need it, typically cutting irrigation time to near zero for the homeowner.

For lawn areas, in-ground irrigation with properly adjusted heads and a rain sensor prevents overwatering while keeping the lawn consistent without manual effort.

The Upfront Investment Pays Off

Low-maintenance landscapes typically require more thought and investment upfront — soil preparation, quality plants, mulch, edging, and possibly irrigation — but the ongoing time and cost savings compound every year. Most of our design clients find that their yard maintenance time drops by 50–70% within two years of a properly designed landscape renovation.
